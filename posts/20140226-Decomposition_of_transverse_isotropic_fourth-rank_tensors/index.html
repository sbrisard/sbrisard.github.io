Decomposition of transverse isotropic, fourth-rank tensors

Published

February 26, 2014

In a previous post, I introduced the fourth-rank spherical and deviatoric projection tensors. Any isotropic fourth-rank tensor is a linear combination of these two tensors; in other words, the space of isotropic fourth-rank tensors (with minor and major symmetries) is of dimension 2. Similarly, it can be shown (Walpole, 1984) that the space of three-dimensional, transverse isotropic fourth-rank tensors (with minor and major symmetries) is of dimension 6. Furthermore; it is possible to produce a convenient basis of this space. This is the topic of the present post, which is mostly based on the paper by Walpole (1984).

Two second-rank projection tensors

It is recalled that transverse isotropy is defined by one single preferred direction. In the present post, \(\vec n\) denotes the unit vector which indicates this preferred direction. Then, \(\mathbf p\) and \(\mathbf q\) are the following projectors \[ \mathbf p=\vec n\otimes\vec n,\quad\mathbf q=\boldsymbol{\delta}-\mathbf p. \tag{1}\]

\(\mathbf p\) is the projector onto the direction of anisotropy \(\vec n\), while \(\mathbf q\) is the projection onto the plane of isotropy (orthogonal to \(\vec n\)). It can readily be verified that \[ \mathbf p\cdot\mathbf p=\mathbf p,\quad \mathbf q\cdot\mathbf q=\mathbf q,\quad \mathbf p\cdot\mathbf q=\mathbf q\cdot\mathbf p=\mathbf 0 \] and \[ \mathbf p:\mathbf p=1,\quad \mathbf q:\mathbf q=2,\quad \mathbf p:\mathbf q=\mathbf q:\mathbf p=0. \]

Tensor product of linear transformations

It will be convenient to introduce a new, Kronecker-like product of two second-rank tensors, which Del Piero (1979) named tensor product of linear transformations, and which will be denoted \(\boxtimes{}\).

As a preliminary note, it should be observed that any fourth-rank tensor \(\mathbf A\) can be viewed as a endomorphism over the space of second-rank tensors, since \(\mathbf x\mapsto\mathbf A:\mathbf x\) is a linear mapping (the convention adopted in this blog for the double dot product has been specified in a previous post). Therefore, the fourth-rank tensor \(\mathbf a\boxtimes\mathbf b\) (\(\mathbf a, \mathbf b\): second-rank tensors) can be defined by how it operates on second-rank tensors \(\mathbf x\) \[ \bigl(\mathbf a\boxtimes\mathbf b\bigr):\mathbf x=\mathbf a\cdot\mathbf x\cdot\mathbf b^{\mathsf{T}}. \tag{2}\]

Writing the above definition in terms of the components of \(\mathbf a\), \(\mathbf b\) and \(\mathbf x\), it is found that \(\bigl(\mathbf a\boxtimes\mathbf b\bigr):\mathbf x=a_{ik}\,x_{kl}\,b_{jl}\,\vec e_i\otimes\vec e_j\), which shows that the components of \(\mathbf a\boxtimes\mathbf b\) are \[ \bigl(\mathbf a\boxtimes\mathbf b\bigr)_{ijkl}=a_{ik}\,b_{jl}, \tag{3}\] from which it results \[ \bigl(\mathbf a\boxtimes\mathbf b\bigr)^{\mathsf{T}}=\mathbf a^{\mathsf{T}}\boxtimes\mathbf b^{\mathsf{T}}. \]

Given four second-rank tensors \(\mathbf a\), \(\mathbf b\), \(\mathbf c\) and \(\mathbf d\), the following identity holds \[ \bigl(\mathbf a\boxtimes\mathbf b\bigr):\bigl(\mathbf c\boxtimes\mathbf d\bigr) =\bigl(\mathbf a\cdot\mathbf c\bigr)\boxtimes\bigl(\mathbf b\cdot\mathbf d\bigr). \tag{4}\]

Indeed, let \(\mathbf x\) be a second-rank tensor. Then, from Eq. (2) \[ \begin{aligned} \bigl(\mathbf a\boxtimes\mathbf b\bigr):\bigl(\mathbf c\boxtimes\mathbf d\bigr):\mathbf x &=\bigl(\mathbf a\boxtimes\mathbf b\bigr):\bigl(\mathbf c\cdot\mathbf x\cdot\mathbf d^{\mathsf{T}}\bigr)=\mathbf a\cdot\bigl(\mathbf c\cdot\mathbf x\cdot\mathbf d^{\mathsf{T}}\bigr)\cdot\mathbf b^{\mathsf{T}}\\ &=\bigl(\mathbf a\cdot\mathbf c\bigr)\cdot\mathbf x\cdot\bigl(\mathbf d^{\mathsf{T}}\cdot\mathbf b^{\mathsf{T}}\bigr)=\bigl(\mathbf a\cdot\mathbf c\bigr)\cdot\mathbf x\cdot\bigl(\mathbf b\cdot\mathbf d\bigr)^{\mathsf{T}}\\ &=\bigl[\bigl(\mathbf a\cdot\mathbf c\bigr)\boxtimes\bigl(\mathbf b\cdot\mathbf d\bigr)\bigr]:\mathbf x. \end{aligned} \]

Eq. (3) shows that even if \(\mathbf a\) and \(\mathbf b\) are symmetric, the tensor product \(\mathbf a\boxtimes\mathbf b\) does not necessarily have the minor symmetries. It will therefore be convenient to define \(\mathbf a\overset{\mathrm s}{\boxtimes}\mathbf b\), as the tensor \(\mathbf a\boxtimes\mathbf b\), symmetrized with respect to the last two indices \[ \bigl(\mathbf a\overset{\mathrm s}{\boxtimes}\mathbf b\bigr)_{ijkl}=\frac12\bigl(a_{ik}\,b_{jl}+a_{il}\,b_{jk}\bigr), \] or, in intrinsic form (\(\mathbf x\): second-rank tensor) \[ \bigl(\mathbf a\overset{\mathrm s}{\boxtimes}\mathbf b\bigr):\mathbf x =\frac12\bigl(\mathbf a\boxtimes\mathbf b\bigr):\bigl(\mathbf x+\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}\bigr) =\frac12\mathbf a\cdot\bigl(\mathbf x+\mathbf x^{\mathsf T}\bigr)\cdot\mathbf b^{\mathsf T}. \tag{5}\]

An identity similar to Eq. (4) can then be derived with \(\overset{\mathrm s}{\boxtimes}\) \[ \bigl(\mathbf a\overset{\mathrm s}{\boxtimes}\mathbf b\bigr):\bigl(\mathbf c\overset{\mathrm s}{\boxtimes}\mathbf d\bigr) =\frac12\bigl[ \bigl(\mathbf a\cdot\mathbf c\bigr)\overset{\mathrm s}{\boxtimes}\bigl(\mathbf b\cdot\mathbf d\bigr) +\bigl(\mathbf a\cdot\mathbf d\bigr)\overset{\mathrm s}{\boxtimes}\bigl(\mathbf b\cdot\mathbf c\bigr) \bigr]. \tag{6}\]

The following identities, involving the second-rank projectors \(\mathbf p\) and \(\mathbf q\) [see Eq. (1)], readily follow from Eq. (3) \[ \begin{align} \bigl(\mathbf p\overset{\mathrm s}{\boxtimes}\mathbf p\bigr):\mathbf p&=\mathbf p,&\bigl(\mathbf p\overset{\mathrm s}{\boxtimes}\mathbf p\bigr):\mathbf q&=\mathbf 0,\\ \bigl(\mathbf q\overset{\mathrm s}{\boxtimes}\mathbf q\bigr):\mathbf p&=\mathbf 0,&\bigl(\mathbf q\overset{\mathrm s}{\boxtimes}\mathbf q\bigr):\mathbf q&=\mathbf q, \end{align} \] and \[\bigl(\mathbf p\overset{\mathrm s}{\boxtimes}\mathbf q\bigr):\mathbf p=\bigl(\mathbf p\overset{\mathrm s}{\boxtimes}\mathbf q\bigr):\mathbf q=\bigl(\mathbf q\overset{\mathrm s}{\boxtimes}\mathbf p\bigr):\mathbf p=\bigl(\mathbf q\overset{\mathrm s}{\boxtimes}\mathbf p\bigr):\mathbf q=\mathbf 0.\]

Besides, the following multiplication table results from Eq. (6)

\(:\) \(\mathbf p\overset{\mathrm s}{\boxtimes}\mathbf p\) \(\mathbf p\overset{\mathrm s}{\boxtimes}\mathbf q\) \(\mathbf q\overset{\mathrm s}{\boxtimes}\mathbf p\) \(\mathbf q\overset{\mathrm s}{\boxtimes}\mathbf q\)
\(\mathbf p\overset{\mathrm s}{\boxtimes}\mathbf p\) \(\mathbf p\overset{\mathrm s}{\boxtimes}\mathbf p\) \(\mathbf 0\) \(\mathbf 0\) \(\mathbf 0\)
\(\mathbf p\overset{\mathrm s}{\boxtimes}\mathbf q\) \(\mathbf 0\) \(\mathbf 0\) \(\mathbf 0\) \(\mathbf 0\)
\(\mathbf q\overset{\mathrm s}{\boxtimes}\mathbf p\) \(\mathbf 0\) \(\mathbf 0\) \(\mathbf 0\) \(\mathbf 0\)
\(\mathbf q\overset{\mathrm s}{\boxtimes}\mathbf q\) \(\mathbf 0\) \(\mathbf 0\) \(\mathbf 0\) \(\mathbf q\overset{\mathrm s}{\boxtimes}\mathbf q\)

To conclude, it is observed that the fourth-rank tensor \(\mathbf{I}=\boldsymbol{\delta}\overset{\mathrm s}{\boxtimes}\boldsymbol{\delta}\) maps any second-rank tensor to its symmetric part: it reduces to the identity tensor over the space of second-rank, symmetric tensors.

Walpole's basis for transverse isotropic tensors

Walpole (1984) shows that any transverse isotropic tensor is a linear combination of the six fourth-rank tensors \(\mathbf E_1\), \(\mathbf E_2\), \(\mathbf E_3\), \(\mathbf E_4\), \(\mathbf F\) and \(\mathbf G\) defined as follows \[\mathbf E_1=\mathbf p\otimes\mathbf p,\quad\mathbf E_2=\frac12\mathbf q\otimes\mathbf q,\quad\mathbf E_3=\frac1{\sqrt2}\mathbf p\otimes\mathbf q,\quad\mathbf E_4=\frac1{\sqrt2}\mathbf q\otimes\mathbf p\] \[\mathbf F=\mathbf q\overset{\mathrm s}{\boxtimes}\mathbf q-\frac12\mathbf q\otimes\mathbf q,\quad\mathbf G=\mathbf p\overset{\mathrm s}{\boxtimes}\mathbf q+\mathbf q\overset{\mathrm s}{\boxtimes}\mathbf p.\]

Multiplication (in the sense of the double product) of these tensors is fairly easy, as shown by the table below.

\(:\) \(\mathbf E_1\) \(\mathbf E_2\) \(\mathbf E_3\) \(\mathbf E_4\) \(\mathbf F\) \(\mathbf G\)
\(\mathbf E_1\) \(\mathbf E_1\) \(\mathbf 0\) \(\mathbf E_3\) \(\mathbf 0\) \(\mathbf 0\) \(\mathbf 0\)
\(\mathbf E_2\) \(\mathbf 0\) \(\mathbf E_2\) \(\mathbf 0\) \(\mathbf E_4\) \(\mathbf 0\) \(\mathbf 0\)
\(\mathbf E_3\) \(\mathbf 0\) \(\mathbf E_3\) \(\mathbf 0\) \(\mathbf E_1\) \(\mathbf 0\) \(\mathbf 0\)
\(\mathbf E_4\) \(\mathbf E_4\) \(\mathbf 0\) \(\mathbf E_2\) \(\mathbf 0\) \(\mathbf 0\) \(\mathbf 0\)
\(\mathbf F\) \(\mathbf 0\) \(\mathbf 0\) \(\mathbf 0\) \(\mathbf 0\) \(\mathbf F\) \(\mathbf 0\)
\(\mathbf G\) \(\mathbf 0\) \(\mathbf 0\) \(\mathbf 0\) \(\mathbf 0\) \(\mathbf 0\) \(\mathbf G\)

The above table must be read as: ‚Äúrow double dot column equals cell‚Äù. For example, \(\mathbf E_1:\mathbf E_3=\mathbf E_3\) and \(\mathbf E_3:\mathbf E_1=\mathbf 0\).

A convenient representation of transverse isotropic, fourth-rank tensors

Walpole (1984) proposes a convenient representation of any transverse isotropic tensor \(\mathbf T\), as the triplet \((A, f, g)\) where \(A\) is a 2√ó2 matrix and \(f\) and \(g\) are two scalars. This representation should be understood as \[ \mathbf T=a_{11}\,\mathbf E_1+a_{22}\,\mathbf E_2+a_{12}\,\mathbf E_3+a_{21}\,\mathbf E_4+f\,\mathbf F+g\,\mathbf G, \] where \(a_{ij}\) are the coefficients of \(A\). The condensed notation \(\mathbf T=(A, f, g)\) shall be adopted. Using the above multiplication table, it can readily be verified that if \(\mathbf T=(A, f, g)\) and \(\mathbf T'=(A', f', g')\), then \[ \mathbf T:\mathbf T'=(A\cdot A', ff', gg'). \] This representation is particularly convenient when it comes to inverting transverse isotropic tensors. Indeed, \[\mathbf T^{-1}=(A^{-1}, 1/f, 1/g). \] Transposition is also straightforward \[ \mathbf T^{\mathsf T}=(A^{\mathsf T}, f, g). \]

Decomposition of fourth-rank, isotropic tensors in Walpole‚Äôs basis

The isotropic tensors \(\mathbf{I}\), \(\mathbf{J}\) and \(\mathbf{K}\) introduced previously can be considered as transverse isotropic tensors. As such, they can be decomposed in Walpole‚Äôs basis. First, the identity tensor \(\mathbf{I}\) decomposes as follows \[ \begin{aligned} \mathbf{I}&=\boldsymbol{\delta}\overset{\mathrm s}{\boxtimes}\boldsymbol{\delta}\\ &=\bigl(\mathbf p+\mathbf q\bigr)\overset{\mathrm s}{\boxtimes}\bigl(\mathbf p+\mathbf q\bigr)\\ &=\mathbf p\overset{\mathrm s}{\boxtimes}\mathbf p+\mathbf p\overset{\mathrm s}{\boxtimes}\mathbf q+\mathbf q\overset{\mathrm s}{\boxtimes}\mathbf p+\mathbf q\overset{\mathrm s}{\boxtimes}\mathbf q\\ &=\mathbf E_1+\mathbf G+\mathbf F+\mathbf E_2. \end{aligned} \] where the identity \(\mathbf p\overset{\mathrm s}{\boxtimes}\mathbf p=\mathbf p\otimes\mathbf p=\mathbf E_1\) has been used. Now, the decomposition of \(\mathbf{J}\) is readily derived from its definition \[ \begin{aligned} 3\mathbf{J}&=\boldsymbol{\delta}\otimes\boldsymbol{\delta}=\bigl(\mathbf p+\mathbf q\bigr)\otimes\bigl(\mathbf p+\mathbf q\bigr)\\ &=\mathbf p\otimes\mathbf p+\mathbf p\otimes\mathbf q+\mathbf q\otimes\mathbf p+\mathbf q\otimes\mathbf q\\ &=\mathbf E_1+\sqrt2\mathbf E_3+\sqrt2\mathbf E_4+2\mathbf E_2. \end{aligned} \]

Finally, the decomposition of the deviatoric projection tensor \(\mathbf{K}\) is found from the definition \(\mathbf{K}=\mathbf{I}-\mathbf{J}\). To sum up \[ \begin{gather} \mathbf{I}=\bigl(\bigl[\begin{smallmatrix}1&0\\ 0&1\end{smallmatrix}\bigr], 1, 1\bigr),\\ \mathbf{J}=\bigl(\tfrac13\bigl[\begin{smallmatrix}1&\sqrt2\\\sqrt2&2\end{smallmatrix}\bigr], 0, 0\bigr),\\ \mathbf{K}=\bigl(\tfrac13\bigl[\begin{smallmatrix}2&-\sqrt2\\-\sqrt2&1\end{smallmatrix}\bigr], 1, 1\bigr). \end{gather} \]

Conclusion

In the present post, a convenient decomposition of three-dimensional, fourth-rank, transverse isotropic tensors as a linear combination of six basis tensors has been proposed. Multiplication (in the sense of the double dot product) of these basis tensors is straightforward. Besides, representing transverse isotropic tensors as a (matrix, scalar, scalar) triplet eases their multiplication and inversion. Walpole‚Äôs basis turns out to be very handy in many situations (including the derivation of the Green operator for strains).
